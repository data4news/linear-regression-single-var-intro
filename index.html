Does a Car's Weight Predict Its Fuel Economy?

An interactive introduction to simple linear regression using the classic Motor Trend Car Road Tests dataset from 1974.

↓   Scroll to explore

Step 1 of 7

The Data: 32 Cars, Two Variables

Each dot represents one of 32 cars tested by Motor Trend magazine in 1974. The x-axis is vehicle weight in thousands of pounds; the y-axis is fuel efficiency in miles per gallon.

Even at a glance the pattern is clear — heavier cars tend to get worse mileage. But how strong is this relationship? Can we put a precise number on it?

That's exactly what linear regression does. Hover over any dot to see the car's name and stats.

Step 2 of 7

The Best-Fit Line

Linear regression draws the single straight line that minimizes the total squared vertical distance from every point to the line. This is called Ordinary Least Squares (OLS).

mpg = β₀ + β₁ × wt + ε

β₀ is the intercept — where the line crosses the y-axis.
β₁ is the slope — how steeply MPG falls as weight increases.
ε is random error the model can't account for.

Step 3 of 7

Slope & Intercept: Reading the Line

Fitting the model to our 32 cars gives:

37.29
Intercept β₀
−5.34
Slope β₁
mpg = 37.29 − 5.34 × wt

The slope of −5.34 means every extra 1,000 lbs of weight corresponds to roughly 5.3 fewer miles per gallon.

The intercept of 37.29 is where the line hits the y-axis — the predicted MPG for a car weighing zero. Not physically meaningful, but mathematically required.

Step 4 of 7

SStotal: Total Variance in MPG

Before we can measure what the model explains, we need a baseline: how much does MPG vary overall?

The dashed line is the mean MPG (ȳ = 20.09). Each orange bar shows how far that car's actual MPG is from the mean. SStotal squares and sums all those gaps.

SStotal = Σ (yᵢ − ȳ)²
20.09
Mean MPG (ȳ)
1126.05

This is the total variance we're trying to explain. The regression model's job is to account for as much of it as possible.

Step 5 of 7

SSresidual: What the Model Couldn't Explain

Now we add the regression line back. Each colored bar is a residual — the gap between the actual MPG and what the model predicted. Green = car beat the prediction; red = car fell short.

SSresidual = Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
278.32
SSresidual

Compared to SStotal = 1126.05, this is much smaller. The regression line fits far better than just guessing the mean for every car.

Step 6 of 7

R²: Putting It Together

R² is simply the fraction of total variance that the model explains — what's left over after subtracting the residual variance.

R² = 1 − (SSres / SStot)
R² = 1 − (278.32 / 1126.05)
R² = 1 − 0.247
R² = 0.753
0.753
R²
75.3%
Variance explained

Weight alone explains 75.3% of the variation in fuel economy. The remaining 24.7% is due to other factors — engine design, aerodynamics, transmission, and so on.

Step 7 of 7

p-value: Could This Be Random Chance?

Even a pattern that looks strong could arise by coincidence in a small sample. The p-value answers: if weight had truly zero effect on MPG, how likely would we be to see a slope at least this extreme?

1.29 × 10⁻¹⁰
p-value for slope

This p-value is astronomically small — far below the conventional 0.05 threshold. The shaded band shows the 95% confidence interval around our regression line.

Bottom line: if weight had no effect on MPG, we'd expect a relationship this strong to appear by chance only once in roughly 8 billion random samples. The relationship is almost certainly real.

Putting It All Together

Simple linear regression gave us a precise, interpretable model. Weight alone predicts MPG with a slope of −5.34 MPG per 1,000 lbs, explains 75% of the variation in fuel economy, and does so with statistical confidence that goes far beyond any reasonable doubt. That's the power — and the beauty — of a single regression line.
